# AI Business — October 15, 2025

Wednesday session with roughly 12 participants, many attending for the first time. The organisers opened by laying out three ground rules: no duplicate topics from other groups, constructive discussion only, and speculations are welcome as long as the group explores what the speculated world would look like rather than arguing over whether the speculation is valid. The format was explicitly a fireside chat rather than a presentation — exchange of real business experience over theory.

---

## 1. OpenAI Product Feed, AEO, and Selling on ChatGPT

The session's theme was OpenAI's newly released commerce tools: OpenAI Apps and OpenAI Commerce. About half the room was already aware of these developments.

**AEO (AI Engine Optimisation):** The group introduced the term as the evolution of SEO — determining whether your product, brand, or concept surfaces in ChatGPT responses. This goes beyond products: people are actively finding ways to inject their personal brand into GPT training data so they appear as results.

**Product Feed Spec:** OpenAI released a technical specification (a JSON-structured document) that businesses must fill out and submit to get their products listed for purchase through ChatGPT. The spec is called "product feed spec" but the documentation is incomplete. There's no guarantee that submitting the form gets your products shown — "it's a fucking mystery as far as how to get your product on GPT."

**Shopify and existing platforms:** One participant raised the practical point that businesses already on Shopify, Shopee, or Lazada probably won't need to do this manually — those platforms will likely build native connectors, similar to how Shopify eventually built its own Google Merchant Center integration. The real opportunity is for service businesses (salons, coworking spaces, cafes) that don't have an existing shop system.

**The commerce vision:** Participants described a future where you sit in your condo and buy carnival food through ChatGPT, which spawns a Grab order with exactly those items. The AI would learn your preferences over time — what you buy daily, where you travel — and proactively suggest shops nearby. One participant connected this to Pulse and similar context-aware platforms that aggregate preference data.

**Can every business fit on ChatGPT?** The group struggled to name a business type that couldn't work on the platform, which led to the conclusion that it's the same land-grab dynamic as Google and every marketplace before it.

## 2. Market Strategy: Micro Over Macro

A participant with ecommerce experience pushed back on macro analysis, arguing it's "generally not useful" for most builders.

**The Symprose Google Feed Connect case study:** When Shopify integrated with Google Merchant Center, an app called Symprose's Google Feed Connect was already charging $5/month to do the same thing. Shopify launched its own free connector, but Symprose didn't go out of business — switching costs kept customers. "Nobody wants to switch to a new feed. You don't know if your ads are gonna get [messed up]." The lesson: speed is the first moat, and if you see an opportunity where five people you know can use something, just launch it.

**Niche language markets:** One participant pointed out that innovations visible in English-speaking markets often take four to five years to reach smaller language markets. Copying something clever from English into a smaller language means zero competition and healthy margins. "If you know 20 Slovak-speaking people, sell them something. Seriously." The group discussed targeting total addressable markets as small as 200k/month rather than chasing venture-scale TAMs.

**Trust beats discoverability:** Rather than asking "how do I get preferred in search results?", the better question is "how do I become the most trusted?" If every Slovak speaker is talking about your product, they'll buy from you before considering alternatives, regardless of algorithmic ranking.

## 3. Southeast Asia as the Highest AI Usage Market

A report was cited showing that Southeast Asia — Thailand and Malaysia in particular — leads global AI usage, not the US. Thailand was already the world leader in daily internet usage (8.5 hours/day on mobile and desktop) back in 2018, per a Hootsuite report. The US has the highest resistance to AI adoption.

**Implications for product builders:** If you're capitalising on ChatGPT's 800 million users, you need to know where they actually are. Building for the US market when sentiment there is "abrasive and high resistance" might be less attractive than building for Southeast Asian markets where adoption is high.

**Language vs. context:** Multiple participants agreed that LLMs handle languages well — one participant chats in four languages interchangeably with no quality issues. The real gap is local data, not language capability. An Israeli participant demonstrated that ChatGPT successfully found hyper-local Israeli sources (a secondhand marketplace that only exists in Israel) and returned them in context, even when queried in English. A Greek participant confirmed the same — ChatGPT found information from a specific Greek city's website (population 100,000) that would never appear in an English Google search. "He must have searched in Greek."

**The dataset problem:** In countries like Peru, the AI speaks Spanish fluently but the local dataset is sparse compared to the US with its Zillow, open government data, and digital-first platforms. "It's not about the language, it's more about context and datasets from a localised market."

## 4. Agents in Practice: Appointment Setting and Visa Applications

**Beauty salon automation (Bangkok):** One participant described building an agent for a facial sculpting clinic in Bangkok. The business made substantial revenue but had operational problems: appointment setting, follow-up calls, date changes, service additions. The agent handled WhatsApp-based communication and appointment management, reducing the phone team from three people to one.

**LINE integration challenge:** Thai businesses heavily use LINE, but its API documentation is difficult to work with compared to WhatsApp. The builder chose WhatsApp partly because the Bangkok clinic served many foreigners. The group acknowledged LINE integration as an unsolved pain point for the Thai market.

**Visa application platform:** The same builder was contacted about creating an agentic platform for Thai visa applications — replacing the work of secretaries and paralegals who verify documents, check requirements, and track law changes. Immigration law changes infrequently (roughly every three to six months), making it a good fit for an agent with periodic knowledge refreshes. One participant jokingly suggested "a calling bot that calls immigration every morning" to check for changes.

**MCP (Model Context Protocol) explained:** The organisers gave a brief explanation for non-technical attendees: MCP is a protocol that lets AI agents communicate with other software. If a legacy service (email, CRM, whatever) has an MCP server, an AI agent can interact with it through natural chat. "You can literally just speak to your [interface] and it will spawn your email."

## 5. The "Build Fast, Then What?" Problem

A recurring tension surfaced: you can build apps quickly with AI, but then what?

**The marketing gap:** "I feel like for me, the 80% of the problem is basically just getting it marketed and doing SEO optimisation, getting people to call these people and sell it or doing TikToks or whatever." The group debated whether failed launches are product problems or distribution problems.

**The 10-friends method:** A participant shared a detailed case study. While running a 40-person ecommerce company, he built an internal profit-tracking app to train employees on which metrics to watch. He knew 10 friends running ecommerce stores, invited them to a villa in Bali, gave them free drinks, and showed them the product. One person bought on the spot. Three talked about it. One friend's friend had a YouTube channel — 500k views on the first video about the product, 100 paying customers within a month. "If you know 10 people who trust you, your problem is solved. Just build something for them."

**The threshold problem:** A counterpoint: not everyone starts with that network. "You already had these 10 people and maybe some of us are just building this stuff and putting it online, but never a single person reaches it." The speaker acknowledged this and pointed to the meetup group itself as a starting point — "You have 20 people right here."

**Community-led selling (the durian example):** One participant described a friend selling durians on TikTok and making significant money, despite durians being cheaper at the market. "They connect with your personality and they wanna help you. They wanna buy from you because they like you and they're willing to pay the added price." This was mind-blowing to the more technically-minded attendees. The broader point: people buy on emotion, not logic, and technical founders consistently underestimate this.

## 6. The Abstraction Theory and Obsolescence Debate

A theoretical discussion explored whether businesses (and people) can become obsolete through AI.

**The argument against obsolescence:** If developers become obsolete, it means there's a cheaper way to do the same work. Cheaper work means businesses save money. Multiple businesses saving money within an industry drives competition, which drives prices down, which creates new dynamics. The speaker framed this as a theoretical model with downstream effects, not speculation: "If this happens, that will happen."

**The IQ bell curve counterargument:** Another participant pointed out that people are already "quite irrelevant" at the lower end of the cognitive distribution — unable to operate a McDonald's ordering kiosk, ineligible for military or state employment due to error rates. AI simply moves that threshold northward, pushing more people out of the productive economy.

**Theory vs. speculation:** The group drew a distinction: speculation is "I believe this will happen," while theory is "if this happens, that will happen." The organiser clarified that the meeting's approach is to explore the worlds that speculations produce rather than debating their validity.

## 7. End-to-End AI in Production: Reality Check

The organisers asked who in the room had successfully completed an end-to-end business process with AI. The answer was sobering.

**Analytics and blind spots:** One participant feeds all business data into AI and asks questions, primarily to find blind spots. "I think AI is really good at finding blind spots." Also using it for creative concept generation — showed a handbag photoshoot where AI-generated concepts guided the real shoot, with the final product matching the AI mockup closely.

**AI product photography:** The group discussed tools that take amateur product photos (shot on a dining table) and place them in professional-looking settings — white surfaces, luxurious aesthetics. While Sora can do this generically, specialised wrappers optimised for product photography are emerging.

**AI video production:** One participant runs a program teaching storytelling-first video creation using AI tools like V03. The focus is on maintaining character consistency across long-form content (five-minute films). He deliberately uses cartoon/animation style rather than realistic AI video to avoid the uncanny valley: "It doesn't look 100%. It looks fucked."

**The authenticity pendulum:** A philosophical observation closed this thread — younger people are returning to point-and-shoot cameras because phone photos are "too perfect." The parallel: when AI-generated content becomes ubiquitous, human-generated content will be valued more, similar to how a copy of Van Gogh is cheap but the original is priceless.

## 8. Feedback and Format for Future Sessions

The session ended with meta-discussion about the meetup format itself.

**What people want:** Practical tactics over theory. One participant proposed a roadmap of standard business topics to cover systematically at each meetup: product validation with AI, landing page drafting, which AI tools to build landing pages, Google Ads generation, MCP integrations for analytics, and end-to-end workflows. "Many of us fail with all these practicalities."

**The tools vetting problem:** The organisers acknowledged that 80-90% of AI-enabled business tools on the market are "essentially just a [ChatGPT] wrapper." Finding tools that are genuinely useful for specific use cases is the hardest problem. They've tried tool roundups before and found most tools do the same thing but also do nothing.

**Suggested format:** A five-to-ten minute intro with AI business tool updates, followed by structured discussion where people share what's actually working in production — cron jobs, APIs, automation pipelines — as both inspiration and proof of concept.
